Aim
The aim of this paper was to provide the reader with a firmer understanding of how information geographies are spread across the world. By explaining and displaying various types of geographic information, statistics and data, the aim of showing how unevenly balanced and spread the geographies of participation and representation are and how some areas are over estimated is well executed.

Information geographies and (im)mutable augmentations
“This fusing of the spatial and informational augmentations that are immutable can result in high stakes as annotations of place emerge as sites of political contestation: with different groups of people trying to impose different narratives on informational augmentations (Zook and Graham 2007)” [1]. Allowing local people to produce content about there own country would greatly make the spread of information geographies more even. However, other countries producing content about another helps the content become less bias and more accurate and truthful. Political influence plays a vital and important role in how local content is produced. Local people may have their lives at steak for produced content that will affect their country in a negative way, which may indicate to the imbalance of content produced in more oppressed countries.
“A restaurant omitted from a map can cease to be a restaurant if nobody finds it. Likewise, how places are presented within informational augmentations fundamentally affects how they are used or brought into being (Graham and Zook 2013)” [1]. I believe the writer uses this statement as a metaphor to show how countries with a poor influence in contributing or accessing online information will become if they don’t start creating, contributing, and accessing digital information and data. The metaphor used is effective as it is what happens in today’s modern society which allows it to be relatable. Most tourists use a Search Engine to locate restaurants nearby that have good ratings, the restaurants that do not have an online profile will have less awareness and representation. Similarly, the countries that have a poor digital platform will soon have little representation and their voice not as powerful as that of other countries.

Geographies of access and enablement
A similar pattern emerges throughout reading, that it is mainly the African countries that are falling behind to the rest of the world, as seen by the graphical representations. It can be seen however that some growth has occurred in certain parts of Africa. However, equal opportunity and growth need to be spread more evenly throughout the African continent. Controversially, it can be seen in some countries, such as India, that although the population density may be large, the internet penetration is significantly lower than some of the countries in Africa. It can be seen today that India has had significant growth in internet penetration since these statistics were released with India becoming a tech hub for futuristic technology and low broadband affordability, whereas African countries have a slow but steady growth rate.
